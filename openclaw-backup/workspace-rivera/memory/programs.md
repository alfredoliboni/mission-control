## Employment Readiness Programs
This file tracks Ontario and local-route programs relevant to adolescent transition planning, employment readiness, and later supported work entry.

## Highest-Priority Programs

### 1. Employment Ontario
- Focus: free employment counselling, resume help, interview prep, skills training connections, youth job supports, apprenticeship information
- Fit for Mateus: strong general entry point as he gets older, especially for local office referrals and youth-disability-aware employment support
- Best timing: later high school, but the family can contact earlier for local program mapping
- Action: find the closest office and ask about youth with disabilities, autism-friendly employment support, and vocational exploration options
- Source: https://www.ontario.ca/page/employment-ontario

### 2. School-based transition planning, experiential learning, and co-op
- Focus: school-led transition planning, job shadowing, volunteer placements, experiential learning, Student Success supports, co-op planning
- Fit for Mateus: probably the most practical next step because school supports can be adapted through the IEP and can start before adult services are involved
- Best timing: now
- Action: ask whether the IEP transition plan includes employment-readiness goals and whether school can offer job shadowing, community-based instruction, or pre-co-op exposure
- Source: Ontario special education transition planning guidance, https://www.ontario.ca/page/special-education-ontario-policy-and-resource-guide-kindergarten-grade-12

### 3. Ready, Willing and Able (RWA)
- Focus: inclusive employment initiative for autistic people and people with intellectual disabilities
- Fit for Mateus: useful later when direct job matching and employer engagement become more realistic
- Best timing: mid-to-late secondary years, not necessarily the immediate first step at 14
- Action: keep on the long-range list and re-check in later teen years
- Source: https://readywillingable.ca/

### 4. Ontario Youth Apprenticeship Program (OYAP)
- Focus: high school co-op credits through placements in skilled trades
- Fit for Mateus: relevant only if he develops interest and tolerance for trade-related environments such as repair, facilities, culinary, or other structured hands-on settings
- Eligibility note: Ontario says students must generally be at least 15, in high school, enrolled in Grade 11 or 12 or equivalent, and have at least 14 credits; some summer-before-Grade-11 participation may be available
- Best timing: later secondary years
- Action: revisit with guidance, co-op staff, and the board OYAP recruiter when age and credits line up
- Source: https://www.ontario.ca/page/ontario-youth-apprenticeship-program

## Summer / Youth Employment Pathways

### 5. Summer youth job supports through Employment Ontario and local community agencies
- Focus: seasonal work exposure, job-readiness workshops, youth counselling, first-job preparation
- Fit for Mateus: good for low-stakes practice if placement quality, sensory fit, and supervision are screened carefully
- Best timing: review each winter and spring for summer intake windows
- Action: search annually from February to April for Mississauga and Peel youth openings
- Source: https://www.ontario.ca/page/employment-ontario

### 6. Municipal and recreation-linked youth opportunities
- Focus: camps, recreation support roles, volunteer experiences, community program involvement
- Fit for Mateus: may offer structured routines and lower-pressure exposure, especially if recreation staff can accommodate communication and sensory needs
- Verification note: exact Mississauga youth-program page should be rechecked seasonally because URLs and offerings change
- Action: review City of Mississauga youth and recreation opportunities each season
- Source to verify: https://www.mississauga.ca/

## How To Prioritize Programs For Mateus

### Best near-term sequence
1. School transition planning
2. Job-shadowing or volunteer exposure
3. Work-readiness skill-building
4. Employment Ontario mapping
5. Co-op / OYAP / direct placement later if fit is strong

### Technology-oriented program fits
- library or school tech support exposure
- device setup or inventory routines
- digital skills clubs or structured media tasks
- office or back-end environments with clear checklists

### Community-oriented program fits
- recreation support tasks
- library setup or shelving
- community volunteer roles with predictable routines
- structured food-prep roles only if smell tolerance is manageable

## Program Selection Rules
Prefer programs that offer:
- predictable routines
- patient supervision
- short trial periods before full commitment
- visual supports or checklist-friendly tasks
- staff who understand disability accommodation
- concrete work tasks rather than vague social expectations

Avoid first placements that depend heavily on:
- fast customer service
- strong smell environments
- heavy multitasking
- unstructured social interaction
- low supervision
